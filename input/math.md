### [Commutative Algebra](/maths_notebook/algebra.pdf)

Commutative algebra is a branch of mathematics that deals with algebraic structures equipped with a commutative operation, most notably rings and modules. The central object of study in commutative algebra is the ring, which consists of a set of elements equipped with two binary operations, addition and multiplication, satisfying certain axioms. Prominent examples of commutative rings include polynomial rings; rings of algebraic integers, including the ordinary integers $\mathbb{Z}$; and $p$-adic integers. Modules are a generalization of vector spaces, where the scalar multiplication is replaced by an action of a ring. Commutative algebra plays a crucial role in various areas of mathematics, including algebraic geometry, number theory, and representation theory. It provides powerful tools for understanding and solving complex mathematical problems, making it an essential tool for mathematicians and scientists alike.

### [Cohomology of Sheaves](/maths_notebook/cohomology.pdf)

Sheaf cohomology is a mathematical concept used in algebraic geometry, topology, and related fields. It is a way of assigning algebraic objects to topological spaces, which encode information about the structure of the space. Sheaf cohomology is particularly important in algebraic geometry, where it is used to study the properties of algebraic varieties and schemes. It is also closely related to homology theory, which is another way of studying the structure of topological spaces. Sheaf cohomology has many applications, including in the study of complex manifolds and the classification of vector bundles.
